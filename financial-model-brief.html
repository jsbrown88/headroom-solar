What the Headroom Solar model needs to answer

A working brief for the financial modelling session with Virginia (Gini). It frames the build, sets out the lines to model, and puts the one question that defines the venture's cash needs at the front: because we buy and own the hardware, how big is the working-capital gap, and how do we fund it?

For: Virginia Filmer-Sankey, Mark Evans-Smith · Prepared by: Jedi · Status: brief, pre-model. All figures placeholders to be set in session.
1

The decisions the model must serve

  1. How much cash does Headroom need to operate, and when does the need peak? (driven by hardware funding)
  2. What does a deal actually make us, by track (owned EPC, funded/PPA, lease) and by add-on (battery, EV, O&M)?
  3. How many deals, of what size, to contribute meaningfully to Mark's £1m mandate in year one?
  4. What recurring revenue (service & maintenance ARR) builds underneath the project income, and how fast?
  5. What is break-even, and what is the downside case if installs come slowly?
2

The headline: the hardware-funding gap

Under the agreed model, Headroom procures and owns the hardware and free-issues it to Get-Services, who supply install labour only. That gives us the procurement margin and the manufacturer warranties, but it puts the cash for the kit on our balance sheet before the client has paid in full. This is the single most important thing the model must size.

The gap to model: the trough between paying the supplier for the kit and receiving the client's final payment. Multiply that by the number of jobs running concurrently and you have Headroom's peak cash requirement. Three levers close it: (1) a larger client deposit that covers the kit cost up front; (2) supplier credit terms (30–60 days) so we pay later; (3) a working-capital facility or director funding to bridge. The model should show peak cash under each.

Cost base & the ring-fence

  • Direct costs: hardware, install (Get-Services), scaffolding, DNO fees, materials insurance, finance costs on working capital.
  • Operating costs: Andy's draw / time, BD & marketing, design tooling, the Headroom intelligence engine, software/CRM, professional fees (legal, insurance, accountancy).
  • Overhead allocation: because Headroom runs as a ring-fenced division of BridgePoint, agree a fair share of BridgePoint shared overhead charged to the Division. This number feeds Division Net Profit, which is what Andy's 50% is calculated on, so set it deliberately.

Outputs we need from the model

  • Monthly P&L and cashflow for 12–24 months.
  • Peak cash requirement and the month it occurs (under each scenario).
  • Break-even: deals and months.
  • Division Net Profit (Andy's 50% base) and the BridgePoint-side split.
  • ARR build: recurring maintenance revenue accumulating month on month.
  • Sensitivity on the three gap levers (deposit %, supplier terms, facility size).
