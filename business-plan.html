Executive summary

Headroom Solar designs, finances and delivers commercial rooftop and ground-mount solar (and battery) for UK businesses in the 50kW to 5MW band. We lead with the boardroom case, not the green case: solar as an off-balance-sheet hedge against energy price volatility and a verified return on a building's most under-used asset, its roof.

We launch fast and light. No new legal entity at outset, no in-house installation crews, no fleet. Headroom runs as a ring-fenced brand and division of BridgePoint AI Ltd. Installations are subcontracted to Russell Smith / Get-Services while we prove pipeline. Sales are accelerated by a tool the competition does not have: the Headroom site-intelligence engine, which pre-qualifies a site's grid headroom, roof yield and payback before a salesperson ever picks up the phone.

Why now

  • Energy is now a board-level risk, not a CSR line. Rising and volatile wholesale prices have moved the conversation from the sustainability officer to the Finance Director.

  • Funded structures remove the capital objection. PPA and lease models let businesses take solar with zero upfront cost, off balance sheet, paying for power at 30–50% below grid.
  • Demand is spiking. Installers report sharp rises in enquiry through 2026 as price shocks hit. The window to move ahead of the crowd is narrowing.
  • We already have the pieces. A credible operator (Andy), a delivery partner (Get-Services), a brand and domain (Headroom), a finance brain (Gini), a dealmaker (Mark), an investor-grade backer (Kevin) and a qualification tool already built.

Brand & legal structure

Headroom launches as a trading brand / division of BridgePoint AI Ltd, not a new company. BridgePoint contracts, invoices, banks and insures the activity. This keeps us inside an existing entity (as you want) and gives us the Headroom name and domain immediately.

The ring-fence

To make a "division" real and to make Andy's 50% meaningful, we ring-fence Headroom inside BridgePoint: a separate cost/revenue ledger, its own bank sub-account or clear cost-centre coding, and a one-page Headroom Division Charter that records ownership of economics, decision rights and the path to a future NewCo.

Watch-out. Running solar EPC inside BridgePoint AI mingles a regulated, insured, physical-works liability with a software/advisory company. Confirm with the insurer and accountant that this does not breach BridgePoint's PI/PL cover or change its risk profile. If it does, the fast alternative is a thin Headroom Energy Ltd later — but we do not need it to start selling.

Giving Andy 50% — without a new entity

The question is really: 50% of what? "The brand" can mean three different things, and conflating them is where partnerships go wrong. Separate them:

What "50%" could meanMechanismSpeedVerdict
50% of the economics (the profit the Headroom division throws off)Written Profit-Share / JV Deed between BridgePoint AI Ltd and Andy: net profit of the Headroom division split 50/50 after defined cost recovery.DaysRecommended start. Gives Andy real 50% upside now, no Companies House, no equity dilution of BridgePoint.
50% of the brand IP (the Headroom name/marks)Co-ownership or licence of the trade mark. Messy while the name also belongs to BridgePoint's wider Headroom tool.WeeksAvoid for now. Keep IP with BridgePoint; licence its use into the division.
50% of a company (shares in Headroom Ltd)Incorporate Headroom Energy Ltd, 50% Andy / 50% BridgePoint, shareholders' agreement.Weeks + ongoing adminThe future state. Give Andy a contractual option to convert his profit-share into 50% equity on incorporation.

Recommended path — "economic now, equity later"

  1. Profit-Share Deed today. Andy gets 50% of Headroom division net profit. BridgePoint's consideration = the Headroom brand, domain, intelligence engine, finance/BD machine and working capital. Andy's consideration = sector expertise, Stratford track record, delivery oversight and origination.
  2. Vesting. The 50% vests over time or against milestones (e.g. 25% on signature, balance over 12–24 months / against pipeline) so a short tenure does not lock in half the value. Add good-leaver / bad-leaver terms.
  3. Option to equitise. Andy holds a written right to convert to 50% of Headroom Energy Ltd on incorporation, on the same economic terms — triggered when revenue justifies a standalone entity.
  4. Decision rights. Define who decides what: Andy leads delivery/technical/operations; BridgePoint (Mark/Gini) leads finance, brand and capital. 50/50 economics need not mean 50/50 control — spell it out to avoid deadlock.
  5. Document it. A short JV/Founder Deed and the Division Charter. Cheap legal now prevents an expensive divorce later.
Why this is the right shape. Andy gets the upside and the title that motivate a founder, today, with no entity gate. BridgePoint keeps its cap table clean and its IP intact. Everyone has a clear, written path to a 50/50 company once the numbers earn it. Kevin and the wider founders sit on the BridgePoint side of the 50% — define their split within BridgePoint's half separately, so Andy never has to negotiate with four people.

The Get-Services delivery model

Russell Smith / Get-Services is our installation labour engine until volume justifies our own capability. The model is deliberate: Headroom owns the client end-to-end and procures and owns the hardware; Get-Services supplies the install labour only. Andy fronts the client relationship throughout and is on site for day one of every install.

How the value splits

  • Headroom keeps: origination, survey/qualification (via the engine), design, finance structuring, the full client relationship, brand, and hardware procurement. We buy the panels, inverters and kit directly, free-issue them to site, and keep the procurement margin and the manufacturer warranty relationship.
  • Get-Services supplies: install labour, crew, plant and access (scaffolding), to our design and specification. They install the hardware we have already paid for and delivered — they do not procure it.
  • Andy: manages the client engagement throughout and attends day one of installation to set the standard, protect the relationship and own QA at the critical first day on site.
  • Commercials to agree: install-only price basis (day-rate vs rate-per-kWp installed), payment milestones, warranty & defects ownership, exclusivity/non-circumvention, and capacity guarantees.
Protect the flank. A non-circumvention / non-solicit clause is essential — Get-Services touches our client at the site visit and the install. Mirror the protection BridgePoint uses on the HH engagement.
Two consequences of owning the hardware. (1) Working capital: we fund the kit upfront, before the client pays in full, so the cashflow model must cover that gap. (2) Risk & insurance: while our hardware sits on site pre- and during install it is our property — we need contractors'-all-risk / goods cover on it, and Get-Services must be liable for any loss or damage they cause handling it. Upside: we hold the manufacturer warranties directly and capture the procurement margin. And: Andy being on site day one must not accidentally make Headroom the principal contractor under CDM — that duty stays with Get-Services; Andy attends as client-side, not as the controlling contractor.

Design the loop, not just the line. Stages 9–11 close back into stage 1. A proven 3-month result (11) becomes a case study (9) and a warm referral (10), which feeds the next enquiry. Most installers stop at switch-on and leave that compounding value on the table. We make the after-care the acquisition channel.
Two ownership flags in the journey. Stage 3 (site visit) and stage 7 (install) are the only stages Get-Services touches the client directly — those are exactly where the non-circumvention clause must bite. And the DNO management (stages 5–6) is a genuine Headroom value-add worth charging for: many buyers fear the grid-connection process, and owning it cleanly is a differentiator.

Service & maintenance — the recurring-revenue line

New installs are lumpy and capital-led. Service is recurring, asset-light and immediate — it earns from day one without waiting for the install pipeline to mature, and it is what turns Headroom from a project shop into a business with a balance-sheet value. Three offers:

OfferWhat it isWhy it mattersRevenue
Legacy installation evaluationtrojan horseIndependent health-check of an existing third-party system: performance vs design, safety & compliance (DC isolators, fire risk, earthing), inverter end-of-life, monitoring gaps.Low-cost front door to a roof we didn't install. Underperformance and safety findings convert straight into a maintenance contract, repowering or battery retrofit.Paid audit + downstream
Maintenance of existing systemsO&M for systems others installed: cleaning, inspection, fault-finding, inverter swaps, monitoring, reporting.Expands our market beyond new-build. Thousands of UK commercial systems are out of warranty, underperforming and orphaned by installers who vanished.Recurring contract
Maintenance contracts on our own systemsTiered O&M on everything we install: monitoring, scheduled service, response SLA, performance guarantee.Protects our performance promise, locks in the client, and is the natural product of the stage-11 review. Sold at the point of install.Recurring ARR
Why this is the strategic prize. Recurring service revenue smooths the lumpy install cashflow, compounds every year, and is what a buyer pays an ARR multiple for — it is how Headroom builds enterprise value, not just project margin. It is also asset-light: inspection and diagnostics are mostly Headroom / engineer time; physical fixes route through Get-Services. Tier it (Bronze monitoring → Silver scheduled → Gold guaranteed-performance) so there is an entry point for every budget.
One requirement to flag. Maintaining other people's installs means inheriting other people's risk. Every legacy system needs a documented condition baseline and a clear scope boundary before we take it on, or we own faults we didn't create. The evaluation (offer 1) is that baseline — so always lead with it.

Go-to-market

Who we target first

Tight ICP for speed: UK owner-occupiers and multi-site operators with large roofs and high daytime load — warehousing & logistics, manufacturing, cold storage, agriculture, leisure/retail parks, care homes, schools/MATs. Daytime load is what makes solar pay, so we screen for it.

The channel

  1. Headroom audit as the front door. Free or low-cost site-intelligence report. It qualifies the prospect and self-selects the serious ones.
  2. Warm network first. Mine BridgePoint, HH, NZP, Kevin's and Andy's contacts before spending on cold acquisition.
  3. FD-led content & outreach. LinkedIn + targeted email with the "roof = liability or engine" hook and the funded/zero-capital angle.
  4. Referral & introducer scheme. Pay for qualified roof introductions (brokers, accountants, commercial agents, facilities firms).
Hot words that convert (from live UK market scan): "zero upfront cost", "off balance sheet", "price certainty / lock your rate", "energy independence", "your roof, working for you", "verified ROI", "funded solar", "hedge against rising bills", "no capital, all the savings", "move before the crowd".
